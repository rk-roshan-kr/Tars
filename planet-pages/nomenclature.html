Exoplanet Classification

Types of Exoplanets
Terrestrial Planets
Rocky planets primarily composed of silicate rocks and metals. They typically have solid surfaces and are similar in structure to Earth, Mercury, Venus, and Mars.
Example: Proxima b, Kepler-186f
Gas Giants
Massive planets composed primarily of hydrogen and helium. They lack solid surfaces and have thick atmospheres with extreme pressure and temperature conditions.
Example: Jupiter, Saturn, HD 209458 b
Ice Giants
Planets with substantial amounts of water, methane, and ammonia ices in addition to hydrogen and helium. They are smaller than gas giants but larger than terrestrial planets.
Example: Uranus, Neptune, Kepler-421 b
Super-Earths
Exoplanets with masses between 1-10 times that of Earth. They may be rocky, gaseous, or a combination of both. Their exact composition is often uncertain.
Example: Gliese 667 Cc, Kepler-442 b
Mini-Neptunes
Planets smaller than Neptune but larger than Earth, typically with thick hydrogen-helium atmospheres. They represent a transition between rocky and gaseous worlds.
Example: Kepler-11f, GJ 1214 b
Hot Jupiters
Gas giant planets that orbit extremely close to their host stars, typically with orbital periods of less than 10 days. Their temperatures can exceed 1000¬∞C.
Example: 51 Pegasi b, HD 189733 b

Habitable Zone Terms

Habitable Zone (HZ)
The region around a star where conditions might be suitable for liquid water to exist on a planet's surface. Also called the "Goldilocks Zone" - not too hot, not too cold.
Earth is in the Sun's habitable zone
Conservative Habitable Zone
The inner and outer boundaries where Earth-like planets could maintain surface liquid water assuming Earth-like atmospheric conditions and composition.
Inner edge: 0.99 AU, Outer edge: 1.70 AU for Sun-like stars
Optimistic Habitable Zone
Extended boundaries that include regions where Venus and Mars might have had liquid water in the past, representing more generous estimates for habitability.
Inner edge: 0.75 AU, Outer edge: 1.77 AU for Sun-like stars
Circumstellar Habitable Zone
The traditional habitable zone around a single star. This is the most commonly referenced type of habitable zone in exoplanet research.
Kepler-186f orbits in its star's circumstellar habitable zone

Detection Methods

Transit Method
A detection method that measures the slight dimming of a star when a planet passes in front of it. This method can also reveal information about the planet's atmosphere.
Used by Kepler and TESS space telescopes
Radial Velocity Method
Detects exoplanets by measuring the tiny wobble of a star caused by gravitational pull from orbiting planets. The star's light is Doppler-shifted as it moves.
Used to discover 51 Pegasi b, the first exoplanet
Direct Imaging
Taking actual photographs of exoplanets by blocking the light from their host stars. This method works best for young, hot, and widely separated planets.
HR 8799 system with four directly imaged planets
Gravitational Microlensing
Uses Einstein's theory of general relativity to detect planets by observing how their gravity bends and magnifies light from background stars.
Particularly effective for finding planets far from their stars

Stellar Classification

Spectral Type
Classification system based on a star's temperature and spectral characteristics. From hottest to coolest: O, B, A, F, G, K, M. Each letter is subdivided with numbers 0-9.
Sun is G2, Proxima Centauri is M5.5
Main Sequence Star
Stars in the stable phase of their life cycle, fusing hydrogen into helium in their cores. About 90% of stars in our galaxy are main sequence stars.
Sun, Alpha Centauri A, Kepler-186
Red Dwarf
Small, cool stars of spectral type M or late K. They are the most common type of star in the galaxy and can live for trillions of years.
Proxima Centauri, TRAPPIST-1, Gliese 667 C
Metallicity
The proportion of elements heavier than hydrogen and helium in a star. Higher metallicity generally correlates with higher probability of planet formation.
Sun has metallicity [Fe/H] = 0.0 (by definition)

Orbital Mechanics

Orbital Period
The time it takes for a planet to complete one full orbit around its star. Measured in days, years, or other time units.
Earth: 365.25 days, Proxima b: ~11.2 days
Semi-Major Axis
The average distance between a planet and its star. For circular orbits, this is simply the radius. For elliptical orbits, it's half the longest diameter.
Earth: 1 AU (149.6 million km), Proxima b: ~0.05 AU
Eccentricity
A measure of how elliptical an orbit is. 0 means perfectly circular, while values approaching 1 indicate highly elliptical orbits.
Earth: 0.017, Mercury: 0.206
Inclination
The tilt of a planet's orbital plane relative to a reference plane (usually the star's equatorial plane or the plane of the sky).
Earth: 0¬∞ (by definition), many exoplanets: 0-90¬∞
